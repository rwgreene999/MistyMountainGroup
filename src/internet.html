The Symbiosis of Privacy and Security

Privacy and security on the internet are inextricably linked, each reinforcing the other in a symbiotic relationship

Consider how privacy enhances security: if malicious actors are unaware of which bank you use, it becomes significantly more challenging for them to target your financial assets. Consider how security enhances privacy: If a hacker can compromise your password but you have 2FA, they still can't get into your bank account.

Information you give away online, and in person, is often used to identify you. For instance: You buy a couch at a furniture store. They require and maintain the following info: Phone number, Home address, History of items bought, Payment information, hours you are home for delivery, etc

If the store is compromise by a disgruntled employee or hacker, then the bad guys gain access to your address, expensive items you bought, and your credit card information.

This is where privacy and security come together. The less they know, the safer you are! This applies to everything you do on the Internet.

Why I Use a Password Manager

Long ago, I created login accounts for Yahoo, Google, Outlook, and a few others. I used the same user ID and password on all these sites to keep things simple. When one of those sites got hacked, the hackers automatically tried all the other sites—as well as several hundred other websites. They gained access to all of my accounts using that one password. Don’t do that!

These days, every website I use has a unique password. Each password looks like something unguessable, such as mgm5Mnuv295NkE or n9zT4qN9Z67MLf. These are practically un-typable passwords—I don’t create them, and I definitely don’t type them in by hand. I use a password manager.

The password manager I use is called Bitwarden. They store, but cannot view, any of my passwords or sensitive information. Their software is open source, which means the code is transparent and security experts have reviewed it. They also hire an independent auditing firm to verify that their site and security measures are robust.

I have two-factor authentication (2FA) enabled, and I can access my vault from any of my computers, phones, or tablets. Bitwarden offers browser extensions for Safari, Chrome, Edge, and others—these automatically fill in passwords for banking sites and many other services. Even if I were to lose my home and all my devices in a disaster, I’d still be able to securely access all of my online accounts.

At the time of this writing, Bitwarden offers both a free version and a very affordable paid version. I chose to pay for the low-cost premium plan, not because I needed the advanced features, but because I felt their excellent service deserved support.

When Picking a Password Manager, Consider:

  • Encryption: Without strong encryption, your data isn't secure.
  • Zero-Knowledge: Even the company shouldn't be able to read your vault.
  • Recovery Options: You should have a secure way to regain access.
  • Company Trustworthiness: Do professionals in the security community trust them?
  • 2FA/MFA: One password isn’t enough—look for multi-factor protection.
  • Cost: Value matters. Some free tools are excellent; others are not.

Some trustworthy password managers worth looking into include: Bitwarden, 1Password, Keeper, NordPass, RoboForm, and Proton Pass.

    Public WiFi Security Risks and VPN Protection

    The Threat of MITM (Man In The Middle) Attacks

    When connected to public WiFi, users are vulnerable to MITM attacks, where malicious actors intercept communications between you and your intended recipient (bank, facebook, ...). This interception allows cybercriminals to:

    • Capture sensitive personal information
    • Steal login credentials
    • Monitor online activities
    VPN: A Shield for Public WiFi Users

    To mitigate these risks, cybersecurity experts strongly recommend using a Virtual Private Network (VPN) when accessing public WiFi networks. A VPN offers several key benefits:

    1. Encryption: VPNs encrypt your data, making it unreadable to potential interceptors.
    2. Anonymity: Your IP address is masked, enhancing your online privacy.
    3. Secure connection: VPNs create a secure tunnel for your data, bypassing potential threats on the public network.

    By employing a VPN while using public WiFi, users can significantly reduce their exposure to MITM attacks and other cybersecurity threats, ensuring a safer online experience in public spaces.

    2FA / MFA (Multi-Factor Authentication)

    More than your password, to keep them out

    MFA stands for Multi-Factor Authentication. This means you use more than one thing to prove who you are. Usually, it is something you know, like your password, and something you have, like your phone or a special code sent to your email. If someone guesses your password, they still can't get into your bank account unless they also have your phone or email. This extra step is what makes MFA more secure for you.

      types of MFA
    • link: This tends ot be an email link you click on to prove you are you!
    • Phone 2FA: This is a code sent to your phone. You have to enter the code in the app or website.
    • TOTP: Timebased One Time Password. This is a code that changes every minute or so. You have to enter the code in the app or website. This code may also come from an app like Google Authenticator, Authy or password manager.
    • Passkey: This is a new method where your 'device' is used to verify you. In some forms, there is no password, just the passkey.
    • Hardware: Hardware solutions are card readers or things you plug into your computer to validate that you have the key, something like a key to your front door.

    Each of these MFA types have drawbacks and advantages. I will look at each in turn.

    • link: Many people seem to acquit using email for various things. This link would require you to have an email access from your phone or browser or windows computer in order to click that link. Typically I don't run email on my phone so I have to be careful with these kind of MFAs.
    • Phone 2FA: Banks seemed to like this process, but it's really insecure. SIM swapping where someone steals your phone number exist. This means they'll also get these messages from your bank. If they already have your password then they can validate themselves with your bank.
    • TOTP: This type of login needs a special app called an "authenticator app". These apps are great for keeping your account safe, but they don’t always work on every device. For example, if you pick Apple’s app, it might not work on a Windows computer or an Android phone. If you choose Google Authenticator, it may not work well with an iPhone. An app like Authy works on lots of different devices. My BitWarden password manager works on all of my devices. When you choose an authenticator app, make sure it can work on all the phones and computers you use, and that it can share the code across them.
    • Passkey: This is a new and very safe way to sign in. It uses your "device" to let you log in, a device like your phone, your computer account, or your web browser. Only that device can open your account, so you don’t have to type a password every time. It just connects by itself! But if you lose or break your device, that can be a problem. That's why some websites give you a backup, like a long code or 20 secret words. Make sure you save that backup, so you can still sign in if your device stops working.
    • Hardware: There's no guessing here. You have your key or you don't. The downside of using a key is they're pretty expensive and you've got to have at least two of them in case one breaks.

    When picking an authenticator app for TOTP or Passkey, consider these things:

    • Does it work on all your devices?
    • Does it share the code across all your devices?
    • Does it have a backup code?
    • Does it have 2fa itself?

    What to consider for a new web site

    Going to a new web site which is asking for signup information> What can you do?

    When you sign up for a new website, thay usually asks you for info like your email, username, and password. Sometimes they also asks 'security questions' questions like "What is your mother's maiden name?" "What is your best friend's name?" or "What city were you born in?" These security questions are used to help the site check it's really you.

    But here's the privacy trick: don't use real answers! Hackers can guess from your your social media or find these answers through previous web page hacks. And never reuse your answers on different websites.

    Also some of these questions have changing answer, like "What is your favorite vacation", or "What is your favorite movie". These change as you progress in your life. Just make up answers and save them in your password manager.

    • Email: Don't use your main email, use a disposable email or throw away email to reduce spam
    • What is your favorite vacation? This changes as you progress in life
    • What is your favorite movie? Again, this will change as you watch more movies

    Save all of your fake answers in your password manager

    Exceptions to this rule:

    • if this is your bank, use real phone, DOB and email so they can find you if necessary
    • on web sites you visit often and if they use phone for 2FA, use real phone
