Essential Microsoft Windows Computer Security Guide

Windows Security: Your Built-in Defender

Microsoft's "Windows Security" is a free, built-in tool, that provides comprehensive protection for your Windows machine.

Microsoft Security is a fine product for home and light business needs. I find that many product reviewers do not review this when comparison to other anti-malware products. It offers:

  • Anti-virus and anti-malware threat protection
  • Firewall
  • Device protection
  • Performance monitoring
  • A few other services
  • It's free and just about as good as any other antimalware.

Removing Pre-installed Antivirus

Just bought a computer? Mosty likely it comes with a pre-installed antivirus program. Consider removing it in favor of Microsoft Security.

New machines often come with pre-installed antivirus software from companies such as Norton or McAfee. These companies pay to have their product preinstalled on new machines. In my experience and in my opinion, these are typically resource-intensive and free only for a limited time. Once the free period ends, they want payments for continued service. I recommend you remove these products and and activate Windows Security from Microsoft instead.

The last time I did computer, I simply uninstalled McAfee (Settings -> Apps -> Installed Apps -> search for McAfee -> “…” pick uninstall ) Once I did this, Windows popped up and suggested I activate their Microsoft Security protection, which I did.

BitLocker: Encrypt Your Drive Data

BitLocker is a technology to secure your drive from someone who steals your computer. If it's turned on, you need to take some action.

ℹ️ What is a 'drive' anyway???? A drive is a storage device like a HDD (hard drive) or SSD. This is how your computer remembers everything after you power off your machine

BitLocker encryptsℹ️ Encryption is the process turning readable information into unreadable information, unless the key is known. your drive, securing your data even if your computer is stolen or if the drive is removed from your computer.

Is it necessary for you to use BitLocker? If your computer was issued to you by a corporation, BitLocker is probably already installed/ You do not need to worry about it, that's your companies responsibility. You most likely enter a password at the power on of your computer before you really get started with a log in.

If this is your computer, you need to consider the implications of BitLocker. If you keep sensitive information on your computer, especially if you travel with your computer, you may consider implementing Bitlocker.

A new computer with Windows installed may come to you already with BitLocker encryption. When you set up your computer, Microsoft encourages you to create a Microsoft account and at that point they may ask to encrypt your drive, or they may just encrypt it. The BitLocker 'unlock key' is attached to your Microsoft account. You can add or remove BitLocker later if you want encrypt or decrypt your drive.

If BitLocker is active on your machine, you absolutely need to get the BitLocker Recovery Codes.. Those keys allow access to your hard drive even if your machine fails, and you move your drive to another computer. Those Recovery Codes are also necessary if you need to reinstall Windows on your machine. And sometimes, you just have to use the keys to get back into your machine. To get these Recovery Codes you log into your Microsoft account and access https://account.microsoft.com/devices/recoverykey Write down the recovery keys, and store them somewhere safe.

To see if BitLocker is active on your machine, go to settings -> security & privacy -> BitLocker

TL:DR;

  • You need to use your Microsoft account
  • Open Bitlocker Drive ( settings -> search for bitlocker )
  • If BitLocker is active, get your recovery codes (see above)
  • If you decide to BitLocker your drive, this is your spot.
  • Remember to back up your recovery codes (not on your machine).

Account Security

There are a few things to know about your computer's security

Consider the following for your Windows machine's security:

  • Microsoft wants you to use their access for login to your computer
  • Be cautious with biometric security like Windows Hello
  • I am not a fan of biometrics, does your fingerprint work if you cut your hand? does you face work if you have a fever? If you are passout drunk, will you wake you the next day finding out someone used your face to log in and ordered 20 pizzas?
  • Will the law or the bad guy hold your phone or windows up to your face, log into your account and view your private comments?
  • Microsoft desires to backup your work to the cloud, which is unencrypted
  • Solutions:
    • Create local accounts
    • Store important files like your bank account pdfs in your folders, not the default folders
    • At this writing, I don't know how to control OneDrive backups, so I uninstalled OneDrive!
