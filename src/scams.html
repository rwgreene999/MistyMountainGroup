The Rise of Modern Scams

In today's digital age, scammers have become more prevalent and sophisticated than ever before. In-person con games have changed to remote manipulation scams where their reach and effectiveness has increased dramatically.

The "Pig Butchering" Scam, both Romance and Crypto

This scam originally targeted individuals through romance schemes. However, with the rise of AI, it has rapidly expanded into areas such as finance, friendships, and the cryptocurrency world.

This scam process is summarized by the following:

  • scammer sends a unsolicited message to a person
  • a relationship is developed between the scammer and the person
  • the relationship develops over time, sometimes over months.
  • eventually, the relationship moves to romance needs help or get-rich-quick schemes
  • Romance: takes advantage of relationship they developed to request money for various reasons, money to solve some immediate problem
  • Crypto: they give you a 'special' opportunity because you are their friend, just invest in some thing they offer for great returns

The Fake Bank Call Scam

Where you contacted by 'your bank' about a large suspicious transaction? Was it really your bank?

This sophisticated scam involves:

  • A call appearing to come from your bank (using spoofed caller ID). ℹ️ FCC on spoofing
  • There is a message about a large, suspicious transaction.
  • It contains instructions to contact them immediately via a provided number or link.
  • This usually is some urgency such as "respond before it's too late"

ACTIONS:

  • Always be skeptical of calls appearing to come from your bank.
  • Do not use the contact options the caller provides such as a phone number or link.
  • Contact your bank or credit card company directly, use the number on the back of the card.
  • Or even drop by the local branch.
  • If it is a legitimate notice, you will find out from your known contact methods.

The Evolution of Bank Scams: From Door-to-Door to Digital

Long before the internet era, a sophisticated form of bank fraud known as the "bank examiner scam" preyed on unsuspecting victims, particularly targeting vulnerable individuals such as the elderly. This scam operated as follows:

The In-Person Approach

Two well-dressed men, posing as bank officials or law enforcement agents, would visit an elderly person's home. They would claim to be investigating a dishonest bank teller suspected of embezzlement.

The False Narrative

The scammers would explain that they needed the victim's help to catch the supposed thief. They would request that the victim withdraw a significant sum of money from their account, which would then be used as "bait" to trap the dishonest employee.

The Deception

The fraudsters would assure the victim that the money would be immediately redeposited, claiming this process would help identify the teller who was manipulating accounts.

The Digital Transformation

In the modern era, this scam has undergone a significant transformation:

Online Reach

Today's scammers no longer need to physically visit their targets. Instead, they exploit the internet's vast reach to contact potential victims through emails, social media, or fraudulent websites.

Wider Net

The digital version of this scam can target a much larger pool of potential victims simultaneously, increasing its efficiency and potential for success.

Varied Tactics

While the core deception remains similar, online scammers often employ more diverse and sophisticated tactics, such as phishing emails, fake banking websites, or social engineering techniques.

The transition from in-person to online scams highlights the adaptability of fraudsters and underscores the importance of digital literacy and cybersecurity awareness in the modern age.

The "Verify You're Human" Scam Version 2

We all know about the "let me send you a number and you read it back to me" scam. Just about every wen site sending a text for 2FA will tell you not to give this number to anyone. But what about the new "verify you're old enough and you are human" where they ask you to perform a command on your computer.

This variation takes advantage of new requirements for age enforcement mechanisms that many web pages have started. As we become used to identifying ourselves as human and/or proving our age, we begin paying little attention to the actions. The inattention is what the scammer uses to take advantage of to install malware.

In one version of this, you are requested to run a command on your computer without realizing it! You receive instructions to prove you are human and old enough. The instructions are:

  1. Press the Win-R (Windows key and the R key)
  2. Press Ctrl-V (The Control key and the V key)
  3. Press Enter

Buy wait! If you do this, what you are really doing is:

  • Win-R - opens a pop up window to run commands
  • Ctrl-V - will paste clipboard contents into the pop up window, that they preloaded malware into your clipboard
  • press enter - runs that command

If you followed these instructions, you just authorized some unknown command to run on your machine. This could be anything, but most likely will load spyware, or some remote control software onto your computer.

Apartment rental application scam

This scam works best when there is a shortage of affordable apartments in your area. You find a good deal on an apartment searching online.

  • But the owner cannot meet you because they are out of town.
  • They are hesitant to show you the place or won't give you the address for ‘safety reasons’.
  • There is a request for a deposit or application fee.
  • Requested payments methods are to phone numbers, paypal or by gift cards
  • Could involve a request that you fill out an online application at your expense.

What might really be happening ...

  • They don’t own the place.
  • When they get your deposit/application fee, they block you and your money is gone.
  • The ‘application’ site will cost you, plus they might get info like your SS number.
Protect yourself
  • Never deposit or apply for a place until you have seen it and know you want to live there.
  • Do not financially commit to a place until met the landlord.
  • Use traceable money transfers, like a check, to make payments.

General anti-scam tips for Protecting Yourself

  • Never share verification codes with anyone.
  • Be skeptical of unsolicited messages.
  • Be aware of online only relationships.
  • Verify financial concerns using official channels (e.g., call the number on your card or by visiting a bank branch).

Remember, staying informed and cautious is your best defense against these evolving scams.

How to detect a scam web page:

In this article, we'll examine a potential scam site discovered on September 30, 2024, and provide tips on how to identify such fraudulent websites.

Example Scam Site

URL: www.Joannst.shop Warning: This site may not be trustworthy for your money or personal information.

This site appears legitimate at first glance, featuring multiple entries, functional menus, and support for multiple languages. It presents itself as a liquidation site for Jo-Ann's, capitalizing on the company's reported bankruptcy process.

Why This Site is very suspicious

  • It just feels wrong intuitively.
  • The URL doesn't end in ".com" (always be suspicious of non-standard domain extensions).
  • Prices are unrealistically low, often about 1/10 of the normal price.
  • Prices are not listed in the standard US format.

How to Check a web page as a probable scam

  1. (1) Use WHOIS lookup:
    • In a browser tab, open: whois.com and enter the web name (in this case, "joannst.shop").
    • Who created this? Check the registrar information.
    • A recent creation date indicates a potential scam.
    • See Whois Data below.
  2. (2) Verify the physical address:
    • Enter the provided address in Google Maps.
    • Use Street View to confirm if the location matches the claimed business.
    • In thie case, the street address was a gas station in Washington state
  3. (3) Look for other suspicious elements:
    • for instance in this case those same items were for sale on the real joanne.com at the real price
